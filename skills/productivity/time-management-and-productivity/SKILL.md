# Time Management and Productivity Guide

## Overview
This skill provides a structured framework for managing daily tasks, structuring learning paths, and building a "Second Brain" using Obsidian. It helps balance academic requirements (RMUTT Big Data studies) with professional projects (trading development, content creation, MP3 store).

## When to Use
- When planning the weekly schedule or daily task breakdown.
- When organizing notes, research papers, or project documentation in Obsidian.
- When optimizing study and development workflows to maintain focus.

## Systems & Methodologies

### 1. Task Prioritization & Execution
- **The Eisenhower Matrix:**
  - *Urgent & Important:* Critical bugs in trading bots, university exam prep, direct client issues.
  - *Important, Not Urgent:* Asset allocation strategy, content planning for Facebook/YouTube, long-term learning (MLOps, Kubernetes).
  - *Delegate/Automate:* Infrastructure monitoring, repetitive data parsing.
- **Time Blocking:**
  - Allocate deep-work blocks (90-120 minutes) specifically for coding and system development.
  - Use pomodoro techniques (e.g., 50 minutes work, 10 minutes break) during intensive study.
  - Dedicate specific blocks for trading logs review and market research.

### 2. Second Brain & Knowledge Management (Obsidian)
- **Folder Structure (P.A.R.A. Method):**
  - `1. Projects`: Active outcomes with a deadline (e.g., "Trading Bot v2 Release", "RMUTT Senior Project").
  - `2. Areas`: Ongoing responsibilities (e.g., "Personal Finance", "Forex Algorithms", "MLOps Infrastructure").
  - `3. Resources`: Topics of interest or reference (e.g., "FastAPI Patterns", "Docker Cheatsheets", "YOLO Architecture").
  - `4. Archives`: Completed or inactive items.
- **Linking & Tagging:**
  - Use wiki links `[[Note Name]]` to connect concepts.
  - Use high-level tags (`#trading`, `#mlops`, `#rmutt`) for indexing. Keep tags minimal and leverage search/links.
  - Create index notes or MOCs (Maps of Content) for broad subjects.

### 3. Continuous Learning (Self-Development)
- **Active Recall & Feynman Technique:** Write notes in Obsidian as if teaching the concepts.
- **Project-Based Learning:** Learn new tools (e.g., Prefect, MLflow, LangGraph) by building small prototypes instead of just watching tutorials.
- **Feedback Loop:** Conduct weekly reviews (every Sunday) to analyze what went well, what stalled, and adjust goals for the upcoming week.

## Common Pitfalls
1. **Collector's Fallacy:** Saving notes/articles to Obsidian without processing, synthesizing, or applying them.
2. **Analysis Paralysis:** Spending too much time organizing tasks/notes rather than executing them.
3. **Overcommitment:** Scheduling every minute of the day without buffers, leading to burnout.

## Verification Checklist
- [ ] Weekly review is completed (usually Sunday).
- [ ] Active project files in Obsidian are updated.
- [ ] Tasks are prioritized using Eisenhower logic before starting the work day.
- [ ] Deep-work blocks are protected from distractions (phone, social media).
